About

Philosophy, imagination, and the ethics of attention.

Maria Gallego-Ortiz is a Ph.D. student in Philosophy at Boston College. Her work explores the role of imagination in moral life, focusing on how individuals perceive, interpret, and ethically shape their world through attention, narrative, and embodied experience.

Drawing on the work of Iris Murdoch and Paul Ricœur, her research investigates moral vision beyond rule-based ethics, emphasizing the importance of perception, subjectivity, and imaginative engagement. For Murdoch, moral life begins not with choice but with seeing—a “just and loving gaze directed upon an individual reality.” Ricœur’s hermeneutic philosophy deepens this inquiry by showing how narrative shapes moral identity, making stories not mere illustrations of ethical principles but constitutive of the moral imagination itself.

Her broader interests include feminist philosophy, phenomenology, and philosophy of religion, especially questions of desire, hospitality, and ethical formation through literature and symbolic experience. She is particularly drawn to the ways in which embodied life—pregnancy, vulnerability, encounter—yields philosophical insight into subjectivity and the ethical relation to the other.

Maria holds an M.A. in Philosophy from Boston College, an M.Litt. in Philosophy from the University of St Andrews, and a B.A. in Philosophy from Universidad de La Sabana in Colombia. She is a recipient of the AAUW International Fellowship and the Clough Center Doctoral Fellowship, and serves as convener of the Boston Phenomenology Circle.
