Teaching Philosophy

I understand teaching philosophy as an invitation to think carefully, read generously, and attend to the world with greater precision. In the classroom, I aim to create a space where students encounter philosophical questions not as historical curiosities but as living concerns that bear on how they understand themselves, others, and the conditions of a shared life. I believe that philosophical inquiry is cultivated through dialogue, close reading, and the patient work of articulating what matters and why. My goal is to help students develop the habits of intellectual honesty, interpretive charity, and sustained reflection that philosophical thinking demands.

Courses

Philosophy of the Person I

Boston College

An introductory course exploring fundamental questions about human existence, identity, freedom, and the moral life. Students engage with major philosophical texts to examine what it means to be a person and to live well among others.

Philosophy of the Person II

Boston College

A continuation of the philosophical exploration of personhood, engaging with questions of ethics, community, justice, and meaning. The course deepens students’ capacity for philosophical analysis through sustained encounter with primary texts.
