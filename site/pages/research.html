Research

Investigating the role of imagination in moral life and ethical perception.

My research is organized around a central question: what role does imagination play in the moral life? I approach this through phenomenology, moral philosophy, and hermeneutics, seeking to understand how ethical perception is shaped by attention, narrative, and embodied experience. Drawing on Iris Murdoch and Paul Ricœur, I explore moral vision as something richer than adherence to rules—an active, imaginative engagement with the world and with others.

This work speaks to broader questions about subjectivity, desire, hospitality, and the formation of ethical life through literature, philosophy, and sacred texts.

Research Themes

Moral Vision & Imagination

Drawing on Iris Murdoch’s conception of moral vision as attentive, loving perception, I examine how imagination enables us to see others and situations with justice and clarity. Morality, on this account, is not primarily a matter of following rules but of cultivating a quality of consciousness—a just and loving gaze directed upon an individual reality.

Narrative & Hermeneutics

Influenced by Paul Ricœur’s hermeneutic philosophy, I investigate how narrative shapes moral identity and understanding. Stories—literary, philosophical, and sacred—are not mere illustrations of ethical principles but constitutive of the moral imagination, enabling us to inhabit perspectives and possibilities beyond our own immediate experience.

Embodiment & Hospitality

I explore how embodied experience—including pregnancy, vulnerability, and encounter—offers philosophical insight into hospitality, subjectivity, and the ethical relation to the other. The lived body is not merely the object of moral reflection but a site of moral meaning, where receptivity and responsibility take concrete form.

Literature & Ethical Life

I am drawn to the philosophical significance of literature as a medium for ethical reflection. Literary texts—from Joyce to contemporary fiction—render moral complexity visible in ways that purely theoretical discourse cannot, offering what Murdoch calls “a training in the habit of attending” to the particular and the real.
